Crime Rate in Victoria




From the annual budget reports by the Victorian Police Commisioner, we can see that the Victorian Police Force has received a steadily increasing budget over the past 10 years. How has this continuous budget increase impacted crime rates across Victoria?




Let's have a look at the crime rate in Victoria over the past 10 years (2012-2021). Due to the large variation in population between each Local Govenment Area (LGA), we'll normalise the rate of offences by the population. Let's compare the rate of offences in Metro Victoria and Regional Victoria.

From the scatter plot on the right, we can see no clear separation of data points from Metro Victoria and Regional Victoria ; rather, all the data points are mixed with each other. This would indicate that Metro Victoria and Regional Victoria are very similar in terms of overall safety.



The blue line is the average rate of offences per 100,000 residents of every LGA in that particular year. On the both graph, we can see more clearly that there has been a steady rise in the average number of offences over the past decade.



It would be interesting to discuss why there was a sudden spike in the average offence rate in 2016.



Note that the vertical axis is not zero. Highlight a region on the bottom graph to inspect more detail!





With all this talk about the crime rate in different LGAs, let's have a look at the crime rate in different Victorian LGA's over the past decade. Use the slider to see the changes over the years.





Comparing 2012 to 2021, it's not difficult to see an overall darkening in the colour of the entire map, corresponding to an overall increase in the rate of offences per 100,000 residents over the years.





Another interesting point to discuss would be the following; are different subcategories of crimes correlated? That is, are LGAs with a high rate of "Crime against the person", which includes offences such as assault and homicide, more prone to having higher rate of "Drug offences"? The Victorian Police categorizes all offences into 6 categories. Let's have a look at their relationships with this scatterplot matrix below. Note we are just using 2021 data here.





We can see that there does seem to be a positive correlation between all types of offences, especially drug offences seemingly being a positive factor influence on the rate of other offences. The category of 'other offences' seems to not have a strong relationship with other subcategories of offences. An interesting note is here, datapoints of Metro Victoria and Regional Victoria seem to have more separation. For example, we can see that Metro Victoria has an overall higher "Drug offence" rate, whilst Regional Victoria has an higher average "Justice procedure offences".
